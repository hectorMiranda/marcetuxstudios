This blog launched in the summer of 2012, which makes it almost fourteen years old as of this spring. I started it partly as a notebook — if I wrote down why a decision made sense, I'd remember the reasoning instead of just the outcome — and partly because the blogs I found most useful as a junior engineer were the ones written by people actually building things, not explaining what the documentation already said. I figured I'd run it for a year or two and either run out of things to say or find a better format.

The fact that I'm still here is mostly the notebook function: the need to articulate something I'm working on in order to understand it well enough to make a good decision. The writing discipline itself changes what I notice. I'll be three paragraphs into a post about an implementation and realize I'm describing a workaround for a problem I should be fixing instead. The blog has vetoed more mediocre decisions than any code review, because explaining a decision in sentences is a different cognitive task than making it.

What's changed most is the level of the problems — less "how do I make this work at all" and more "how do I make this work correctly under the conditions I can't control." The identity work at AmaWaterways is a good example: the standard is clear, the implementation is not hard, the correctness across failure modes is where the real thinking goes. Fourteen years of accumulating failures teaches you to find that layer faster. I'll take the trade.
