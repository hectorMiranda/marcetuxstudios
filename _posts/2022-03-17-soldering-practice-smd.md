Surface-mount components scared me for years. Through-hole is forgiving — the leads go
through a hole and you have both hands to work with. SMD parts sit on pads the size of
a pen mark and you're moving hot iron on a surface with no mechanical registration.
This month I committed to actually getting good at it, spending evenings on practice
boards with 0805 and 0603 resistors until it stopped feeling like a gamble.

The technique that unlocked it is drag soldering for multi-pin components: flow a bit
of solder on one pad, place the chip, reflow that first joint to lock it in place, then
add flux paste to the remaining pins and drag the iron across them. Surface tension
pulls the solder into each joint and capillary action prevents bridges if you keep the
iron moving and use the right amount. For discretes, tweezers in one hand, iron in
the other, a dot of flux, and the 0805s go down reliably now.

The tools matter more than I admitted when I was using a cheap tip. A fine conical tip,
temperature-controlled iron around 330°C, good flux paste, and quality solder make the
difference between "this usually works" and "this reliably works." The investment in a
decent station paid back in confidence the first session I used it. Same principle as
every other tool: the right tool at the right temperature, not the cheapest one that
technically does the job.
