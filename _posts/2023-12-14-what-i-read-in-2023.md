The between-roles reading gap in November gave me time to go back and actually read
the papers that explain why the tools I was using work. The ones that changed my
understanding in 2023: "Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Knowledge-Intensive NLP
Tasks" (the original RAG paper from Meta, 2020, which I should have read sooner),
"Attention Is All You Need" (read it twice; I understood it better the second time),
and "RLHF" literature — the InstructGPT paper that explains why instruction-tuned
models behave better for users than raw language models.

On the Rust side, the Async Book (the unofficial async Rust guide at rust-lang.github.io)
and the Rustonomicon (for the unsafe Rust sections) filled in parts of the language I'd
been working around rather than understanding. The "Programming Rust" book by Blandy and
Orendorff is the one I recommend first, but the Async Book is where the mental model
for futures and executors actually comes from.

The book that recalibrated my career thinking: "The Staff Engineer's Path" by Tanya
Reilly. I was already post-IC-ladder in the architect/lead role at CNB, but the
framework for thinking about scope, influence, and what "senior" actually means when
you're not just the person with the most tenure is the clearest I've read. Recommended
to anyone who's been doing this long enough to wonder what the next growth axis is.
