I've been sitting with the "hiring for culture fit" phrase since the first engineer
started in October, because at a two-person engineering team the phrase is almost
circular — we are the culture. A new hire doesn't fit an existing culture; they change
it. That's a different framing than what I learned from working at larger companies
where culture was a thing that existed before you arrived.

What I'm actually evaluating when I think I'm evaluating "culture fit" is: does this
person operate well in ambiguity, and are they honest about what they don't know? The
first is critical because we don't have product specs that are complete — we have
direction and priorities and stakeholder feedback that's constantly evolving. The second
is critical because a small team can't afford the compounding cost of someone who guesses
when they should be asking. Wrong assumptions in a 200-person company are corrected by
the people around you. In a two-person team they compound until they're a production
incident.

The screening question I've started using that reveals both: tell me about a decision
you made that turned out to be wrong, and what you did when you found out. Not what
you should have done in hindsight — what you actually did. The engineers who answer
that question specifically are the ones I want to work with. The ones who pivot to what
they should have done are telling me something too, just not the thing I asked.
