Six months between full-time roles now, which is the longest gap in twelve years.
I said this would be intentional and it mostly has been, but I want to be honest
about what that means in practice. The financial math works — consulting covers it —
and the learning density is genuinely higher than a full-time role typically allows.
What I didn't account for is the social infrastructure a job provides without naming
it. The daily conversation, the shared problem, the context you don't have to rebuild
every meeting. Working alone is not lonely exactly; it's a different kind of social.

The tutoring has been the best surprise. I went into it thinking it would be a
revenue line that didn't require sales calls. It turned out to be the thing I look
forward to most in the week. The students are serious and the problems are concrete:
this code doesn't work, this concept doesn't make sense yet, this career decision
feels impossible. There's a feedback loop that consulting doesn't have — the student
either understands it or they don't and you find out immediately.

On the technical side I'm more current on AI tooling than I would have been in any
full-time role I'd have taken six months ago. That's not a brag; it's an observation
about how gaps create space to learn. By the end of the year I want to be running
something that has a name and an address, not just "consulting." The studio idea
keeps coming up. That's a sign worth listening to.
