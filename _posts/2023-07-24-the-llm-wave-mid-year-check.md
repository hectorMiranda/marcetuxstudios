Six months into what I've been calling the LLM year, and the tempo of releases is
something I've only seen twice before: during the Docker/microservices wave in
2014–2015 and during the web3 peak in 2021. The difference is the user counts. Docker
in 2014 was a developer tool with a niche adoption curve. LLMs in 2023 crossed 100M
users faster than any consumer product in history and the user base is not developers.
That's different.

The blockchain comparison is the one I keep returning to because I was there. The hype
mechanics look similar: breathless coverage, VC money flooding in, projects launched
with a whitepaper and a token. The meaningful difference is that LLMs have demonstrated
value in a way blockchain mostly didn't. Summarizing a document, drafting an email,
explaining a stack trace — these are actual tasks real people were paying alternatives
to accomplish. The technology is solving problems that exist.

That said, the "AI will replace all software engineers" discourse is having the same
effect on my blood pressure that "every business will be on the blockchain" had in
2018. The tool is real. The hyperbole is not. My calibration: this changes how I write
code, how I search, how I draft documents. It doesn't change what a senior engineer
knows or what judgment in a design review is worth. The senior skill gets amplified.
The junior steps that could be automated get automated.
