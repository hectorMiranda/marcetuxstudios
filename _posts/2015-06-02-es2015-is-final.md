The ECMA-262 standard committee ratified ES2015 on June 17th, which is the official end
of the "should I bet on this?" question. I've been using ES6 features via Babel since
January; what changed is that "ES2015" is now a named, stable standard rather than a
moving target. Babel's `es2015` preset covers the whole thing, and the language features
you've been treating as preview are now spec.

The features I reach for daily after six months of use: **arrow functions** eliminate
the `var self = this` dance that polluted callbacks. **Destructuring** makes pulling
fields from objects and arrays into variables read like the structure you're extracting
from. **Template literals** end the string-plus-variable concatenation that made reading
JavaScript harder than it needed to be. **`let` and `const`** replace `var` entirely —
`const` for things that don't rebind, `let` for things that do, `var` for nothing.

The spec also includes `class`, modules, generators, `Map`/`Set`, and `Symbol`. Not
everything is a daily tool; some of it I haven't touched. But the core language got
genuinely better and the Babel workflow means I don't have to wait for browser support
to converge. The moment a standard is ratified is the moment "I'll start when browsers
support it" becomes the excuse it always was.
