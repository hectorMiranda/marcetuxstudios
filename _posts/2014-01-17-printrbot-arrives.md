The Printrbot Simple came in a flat-rate box, partially assembled, and I spent most of
Saturday getting my first real 3D print to stick to the bed. The machine is a RepRap
derivative — open-source design, wooden frame, direct-drive extruder — and it cost about
a third of what the Makerbot crowd pays for a smoother out-of-box experience. I knew
what I was getting into. Mostly.

Bed leveling is the initiation rite. The nozzle has to pass across the bed at a nearly
constant gap — close enough that the first layer squishes into the surface, far enough
that it doesn't drag. Too far and the filament doesn't stick; too close and you grind
the bed. The test print is a 20mm calibration cube that reveals every problem in the
first five layers. I went through a dozen calibration runs before a cube came out square
with no warping.

The first print that actually mattered was a Raspberry Pi case I'd been downloading for
months, printed in white PLA over about two hours. It's not pretty — the infill pattern
is visible through thin walls and the top layer has a slight ridge — but it's mine, and
it replaced a piece of electrical tape holding two boards together on my desk. The
machine paid for itself in my head the moment that lid clicked on.
