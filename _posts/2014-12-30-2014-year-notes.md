2014 was the year I changed stacks and, unexpectedly, didn't break anything important.
The first half was Edgecast winding down its independence under Verizon, building out
the Angular/Web API codebase while watching the org chart rearrange around us. The
second half was Ruby on Rails and PostgreSQL and Couchbase and AWS, at a company where
the product is people's love lives, which focuses the mind in an interesting way.

The technical things I'll carry forward: PostgreSQL's EXPLAIN ANALYZE replaced SQL
Server's execution plan viewer but taught the same lesson — read the plan before guessing.
Couchbase document modeling forced me to think about data access patterns before data
schema, which is a better order of operations than I was using in relational-only
thinking. Service objects in Rails and the service layer pattern in C# are the same
idea in different clothes. The patterns that matter transfer; the syntax is the easy
part.

The thing I didn't expect to care about this year was the operational side. Heartbleed
in April, the SQS idempotency work, the VACUUM investigation in December, the CloudWatch
custom metrics — all of it is about understanding what happens when the code you write
runs unsupervised at 2 AM. I care more about that than I did in January, and I think
I'll care more still in 2025. The best engineering is boring in production. That's the
goal.
