2022 was the year I became a Rust developer. That statement would have seemed unlikely
in January — I'd poked at Rust before, liked the ownership model in the abstract,
bounced off the borrow checker in practice. Twelve months of writing production contract
code in Rust is a different kind of teacher, and what I have at the end of it is not
just competence with the language but a shift in how I think about correctness. The
type system, the lifetime model, the explicit error handling — they're not Rust-specific
habits anymore. They're just how I think about the shape of correct programs now.

The year had a lot of firsts: first smart contract shipped to mainnet, first game jam
(second place, Sydney, forty-eight hours, Unity 2D and Casper tokens, no regrets), first
sustained encounter with image generation tools that I couldn't dismiss. The Stable
Diffusion and ChatGPT moments both hit the same way — a set of prior assumptions
becoming visibly wrong faster than I expected. I don't know exactly what that means for
the next year, but I think I'm paying the right kind of attention.

The contract is live on mainnet. The LoRa mesh is running. The Rust is fluent. Three
things I couldn't have said a year ago, and they matter to me in proportion to how hard
they were to earn. See you in 2023.
