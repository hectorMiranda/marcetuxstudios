October landed with more clarity than September. The blockchain reading went from
"I'm curious about this" to "I want to work on this," which is a different sentence
with a different set of obligations attached. The Rust error handling model clicked
in the way the borrow checker clicked in July — slowly and then all at once, like
a lens that took weeks to focus and then everything behind it was sharp.

At work, the .NET 6 RC went into the new microservice and it's been running in
staging for two weeks without drama. Minimal APIs is production-ready and I'd ship
it without hesitation on any greenfield service at this point. The twelve-factor
post was more opinionated than I usually write and I stand by the ranking: those
three factors are where real projects drift the most.

I've been talking to people in the Rust + blockchain space. Not evangelists —
engineers who are actually building things with smart contracts and distributed
ledgers. The problems they're working on are genuinely hard: formal verification
of contract code, latency-sensitive consensus clients, cross-chain interoperability.
I have the systems programming background to be useful. I do not yet have the
distributed-ledger depth. That gap is fixable, and I'm working on it with some
urgency. November is going to be a planning month.
