The first week at the new job is always the same in the important ways: too much to learn
all at once, names attached to faces you haven't solidified yet, a codebase that has
years of context you don't have. The specifics here are different from anything I've
worked in. Ruby on Rails. PostgreSQL. Capistrano. AWS. Consumer-facing product with
real users who care when it's slow.

The Rails app is large. I mean that in a specific way — large in the ActiveRecord models,
where years of feature additions have accumulated in a handful of models that know
everything about the application domain. The `User` model is the kind of model you write
when you're not thinking about where complexity belongs. That's not a criticism;
it's a description of what happens in any product that grows. Understanding what's there
before suggesting what should change.

The team is small, the shipping cadence is fast, and the on-call rotation starts for me
in week three. The constraint of being on-call for a consumer product is that "slow" is
a customer complaint, not a ticket. I prefer that kind of feedback loop. The .NET
detox is going to take a few weeks — I keep reaching for C# patterns and finding
the Ruby equivalent is different in ways I haven't fully internalized yet.
