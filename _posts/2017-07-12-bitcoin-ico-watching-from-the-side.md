Bitcoin crossed $2,000 in May, then $3,000 in June, and the velocity of the ICO market
this summer is something I'm watching with an engineer's skepticism and a human being's
curiosity. I've had Bitcoin since 2013 — not a lot, more of a technology experiment
than an investment — and the distance between "this is an interesting distributed
consensus mechanism" and "this token represents fractional ownership of a decentralized
cat-rating protocol" is a distance I can't evaluate without more time passing.

What I can evaluate is the engineering. Ethereum smart contracts are Turing-complete
programs that run on a global distributed VM, which is genuinely interesting. The DAO
hack last year — where an attacker drained $60 million by exploiting a reentrancy bug
in a smart contract — is a useful reminder that "the code is the contract" has teeth
in both directions. The immutability that makes the system trustworthy is the same
property that makes a bug unforgivable. You don't get to deploy a hotfix.

I'm not investing in ICOs. I don't have enough information to evaluate most of the
projects, which means I'd be speculating, not investing. The engineers I respect most
who are building in this space are careful to describe what the technology can and
can't guarantee. The ones who aren't careful with that distinction are the ones who
get written about later in the context of things going wrong. Watching carefully,
staying close to the fundamentals.
