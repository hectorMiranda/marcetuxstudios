Year-end accounting on what actually shipped versus what generated conference talks.
Angular 2 and TypeScript 2.0 are both final and both good — the combination is the best
TypeScript web development experience I've had. .NET Core 1.0 is real and running on
Linux in containers; the ecosystem has gaps but the platform is solid. Let's Encrypt
went from beta to general availability and there is now no excuse for HTTP-only internal
services. Docker is mainstream in a way it wasn't in 2015 — not just "some teams are
experimenting" but "this is how software is packaged."

The things that generated more talk than shipping: Kubernetes is ascendant but "using
Kubernetes" still means most teams are using managed Kubernetes at a company that has
an SRE team rather than running it themselves. Progressive Web Apps are real technology
but Safari's continued non-support makes them genuinely limited for anything that needs
iOS. Serverless is useful for event-driven glue and poorly suited for almost everything
else, regardless of what the blog posts say.

The surprise: Vue 2.0 is genuinely competitive with React and Angular in a way that
Vue 1.x was not. The 2016 front-end landscape has three serious options for the first
time. ES2016 proved the annual-small-release cadence works and ES2017 is going to
continue it. KiCad migrated from Eagle on my bench this year without major incident.
The ESP32 is the hardware platform for the next few years.
