The Printrbot turned one year old this month. The honest accounting is more interesting
than the optimistic promise I made when it arrived.

The prints I actually use: six ESP8266 enclosures (these have been genuinely useful —
they protect the modules and mount neatly on walls), two Raspberry Pi cases, a filament
guide bracket, four cable organizers, and a replacement bracket for the desk lamp arm.
The prints that are decorative objects I told myself were useful: three calibration cubes,
a phone stand that I replaced with a metal one, and a vase my partner described as
"abstract." The print success rate is around 85% — the failures are mostly first layers
that didn't stick and two PETG prints that warped on the corners despite the brim.

The unexpected value is design iteration. Designing the ESP8266 enclosure in OpenSCAD,
printing it, noticing the screw boss is two millimeters off, fixing the SCAD file, and
printing again takes forty-five minutes total. That loop — design, print, assess,
iterate — is faster than ordering a custom part and waiting two weeks, and it's changed
how I approach hardware design. The Printrbot is a design iteration tool more than a
manufacturing tool. That's probably the right frame for what home 3D printing is, at
this stage.
