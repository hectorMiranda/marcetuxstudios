Three weeks into this and the biggest adjustment isn't the work — it's the calendar.
When you're an employee, someone else holds the context for what needs to happen next.
A standup, a sprint board, a Slack ping: there's always a next-action in the
environment. Consulting solo means the environment doesn't produce next-actions; you
have to. That sounds obvious but it costs more energy than I expected.

The second change is client communication. Inside a company you can be imprecise
because people share context. With a consulting client the context gap is wide and
imprecision compounds quickly. I've started writing more explicit summaries after
every working session — what I did, what I found, what the three options are — and
the clients who've seen it say it's more clarity than they get from most contractors.
That's a low bar, but it's also the bar that matters.

What hasn't changed: the work itself. A messy data pipeline is still a messy data
pipeline; a poorly scoped API is still a poorly scoped API. The problems don't
care about the employment arrangement. If anything, moving between clients quickly
exposes how many "this is how we do it here" decisions are actually "the first
person to touch this made a choice and it calcified." Fresh eyes cost money. They're
usually worth it.
