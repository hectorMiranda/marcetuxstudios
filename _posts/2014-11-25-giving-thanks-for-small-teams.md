Thanksgiving week is a good time to think about the work environment rather than just
the work. Five months at Spark and the thing I'm most grateful for is the team size.
There are six engineers. Six. I know what every production system does and why. I can
talk to the person who made every significant architecture decision and get the actual
context, not the ticket summary.

The operational clarity that comes from a small team is underrated. In a large
organization, "who owns this service" is a real question that sometimes takes days to
answer. Here, the answer is always obvious. That clarity costs coordination overhead
on some tasks — six people make decisions differently than sixty — but it pays back
in debugging speed. When something breaks at 2 AM, the person on-call understands the
full context of every system they're being paged about. That is not common and it is
worth protecting.

The thing I'm actively learning from the small-team environment is when to formalize
and when to stay loose. Six people don't need the process overhead that sixty people
need, but they do need conventions — naming, commit message format, deployment
checklist — that prevent small teams from becoming chaotic. The right process for a
team of six is invisible when things are going well and painful when it's missing.
Getting that right is the ongoing project.
