Twelve months, one extraordinary disruption, a lot of infrastructure rethinking. The
year started in an office in downtown LA and ended at a desk in the second bedroom.
The work continued, differently.

The technical arc: the IaC foundation built in January and February (Terraform, AKS,
Key Vault) carried the remote transition in March better than I expected. The security
hardening — image scanning, network policies, OPA, mTLS, now supply chain — built
through the year into something the security review at year-end actually accepted.
.NET 5 shipped in November and it's as good as the six months of previews suggested.
Blazor WebAssembly is in production. GitHub Actions is the default pipeline.

The home lab went from a single Pi to a cluster with proper distributed storage,
a metrics stack, and hardware sensors reporting solar and energy data to a dashboard
that's become part of the daily rhythm. The ghost load is gone. The ESP32 solar
reader is the best fifteen-dollar firmware project of the year.

The thing I keep coming back to from 2020: async-by-default isn't just an
architecture pattern for distributed systems, it's an operating model. Documentation
that doesn't require someone to be in the room. Runbooks that work at 2 AM.
Pipelines that run without babysitting. These were engineering choices before March;
after March they were requirements. Build the kind of infrastructure that works
whether or not you're present. That's the 2020 lesson.
