The first formal CTO task at Go RN is a hiring decision I've never been the final
decision-maker on before. There are three candidates at different stages of evaluation
and the question isn't purely technical — it's who can grow into this company's needs
over the next 18 months, not who has the longest current skills match. That distinction
took me a few days to internalize.

My current filter for the role: Can they write code I'd be comfortable deploying on
a Friday afternoon? Do they understand why something broke when it breaks, or do they
just apply fixes from Stack Overflow until something stops? And specifically for a two-
person team — can they work well in the absence of clear requirements, because
requirements at a seed-stage startup are aspirational documents that change on Tuesdays?
The last filter is the one that most resumes don't signal.

The interview process I settled on: a code review exercise where I show them a real
piece of the Go RN codebase (anonymized, with personal data removed) and ask them to
review it as if I was their junior. Not a whiteboard algorithm — I don't care if they
can invert a binary tree in 20 minutes; I care if they can read unfamiliar code, form a
useful opinion about it, and communicate that opinion in a way I'd want to receive. The
person who says "here's a bug, here's what I'd change, and here's what I'd leave alone"
is the person I want.
