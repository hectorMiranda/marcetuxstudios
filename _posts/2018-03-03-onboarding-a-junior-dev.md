The junior backend hire started Monday, and the first thing I realized is that I
have never written down how the services fit together. There's a diagram in my head
that's been accurate for eighteen months and lives nowhere else. Watching someone else
navigate the codebase for the first time is an efficient way to discover which of
your assumptions are not actually encoded anywhere.

The first day I handed over two things: a system diagram I drew over the weekend (boxes
and arrows, services and queues, nothing fancy — draw.io exported to a PDF), and a
list of five good first issues with enough context that a reasonable person could start
without a two-hour briefing. That second part is harder than it sounds. "Good first
issue" in a startup usually means "not on the critical path but genuinely matters,"
and finding those without manufacturing fake work takes some thought.

What I learned from my own early jobs is that the fastest way to productive is to
push the first PR, get feedback, and push a second one. So I said: any PR in the
first week is a win, even if I end up rewriting half of it in review. The goal is
the feedback loop, not the output. We had a real commit merged by Thursday, and that
baseline of "I have shipped something" matters more to a junior engineer than I think
most senior people remember.
