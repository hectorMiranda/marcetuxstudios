The wall-powered ESP8266 temperature sensor has been running for six weeks without
issue. This month I wanted to make a battery-powered version for the spot in the
apartment where there's no outlet — a sliding door sensor. The 80mA during transmission
that was fine for wall power would drain a 1000mAh LiPo in under two weeks. Deep sleep
changes the math.

Deep sleep puts the ESP8266 into a state where it draws about 20 microamps — from 80mA
to 0.02mA is a four-thousand-fold reduction. The chip wakes up, connects to WiFi, sends
a reading, and goes back to deep sleep. The tricky part is the wake-up: deep sleep
requires GPIO16 to be wired to RST with a jumper. When the sleep timer expires, GPIO16
pulses low, which pulls RST low, which triggers a hard reset and re-runs the setup code.
The `ESP.deepSleep(60e6)` call sets the sleep duration in microseconds; 60e6 is sixty
seconds.

The real power cost is the WiFi reconnect — about 300ms of activity at 80mA, which is
the dominant energy draw in each cycle. Calculated over an hour at sixty-second intervals:
60 cycles × 300ms × 80mA = 1440mAs, plus the sleep baseline. On a 2000mAh battery
that's months. The sensor has been running on a LiPo taped to the door frame for two
weeks and the battery is still above 80%.
