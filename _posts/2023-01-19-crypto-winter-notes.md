The FTX collapse in November made something that was already uncomfortable into
something that's hard to ignore. Crypto winter is an industry term for a bear market,
but from inside a blockchain company it means layoffs at peer organizations, project
cancellations, and a lot of quiet re-evaluation of whether the applications we're
building have real users or just incentive structures.

I'm still at CasperLabs, still writing Rust, still shipping contract tooling. The work
is technically interesting — the language, the VM model, the correctness requirements of
a distributed ledger — and I don't regret the last year of deep Rust. But the
enthusiasm-to-skepticism ratio I bring to the product layer is different now than it
was in early 2022. Web3 promised to move the ownership of data back to users; mostly
it moved it to a different set of intermediaries with worse UX and higher latency.

What I keep watching is the LLM space, which has the opposite energy right now: real
users, real use cases appearing weekly, genuine surprise at capability. That's the
feeling that was in the blockchain space in 2016 and evaporated. I don't know what
that means for my career yet, but I'm paying attention.
