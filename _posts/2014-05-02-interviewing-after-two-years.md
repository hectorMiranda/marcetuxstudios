The active part of job searching started in earnest this month and I had forgotten how
disorienting the interview process is when you've been building things at depth for two
years. The work is concrete — I can point at a dashboard that handles a million requests
a day, at a caching layer that halved origin load, at a directive that replaced five
copy-pasted templates. The interview asks me to reverse a linked list on a whiteboard in
twenty minutes.

I understand why technical interviews are the way they are, mostly. Pattern recognition
under pressure does predict something about how you handle novel problems. But the gap
between "what I do every day" and "what the interview tests" is wide enough that I
scheduled a week of problem-solving practice before the first round. LeetCode wasn't a
thing yet so it was old-fashioned: algorithm books and practice problems on paper.

The company I'm most interested in after three first-rounds is Spark Networks — online
dating, LA-based, smaller team than the CDN world, Ruby on Rails back end, a product
that actual consumers use at scale. The stack is completely different from what I've been
doing. That's a feature, not a bug. I've been in .NET/IIS land for two years; I want to
know what else is out there before the stack is my identity.
